The Structural Gap

Cocoa-producing countries export raw beans while the most profitable parts of the value chain—grinding, refining, manufacturing, branding, and retail—are concentrated elsewhere.

Our Solution: Move beyond production alone toward local processing and value-added manufacturing in Ghana.

Ghana's Policy Response

Beginning with the 2026/27 crop season, Ghana has directed that at least 50% of cocoa beans be processed locally.

These reforms increase domestic value addition, support local processors, revive industrial capacity, and reduce dependence on external financing.

The Diaspora as the 17th Region

Ghana's "17th Region" concept mobilizes diaspora participation in national development. Treated as a formal partner—not just a symbolic audience—the global African diaspora brings capital, expertise, and innovation to sectors like cocoa where it can bridge the gap between national ambition and industrial expansion.

Capital

Diaspora investment in Ghana's cocoa-processing industry as a strategic vehicle for national development.

Expertise

Skills transfer and technical knowledge to strengthen Ghana's industrial capacity in cocoa manufacturing.

Innovation

Business networks and market access to create sustainable value chains anchored in Ghana.

About The Chocolate Future

Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire produce over 60% of the world's cocoa, yet capture only 2-4% of the value. The Chocolate Future bridges this gap by connecting diaspora capital to Ghana's local processing agenda, creating jobs, strengthening industrial capacity, and building a more resilient cocoa economy.
